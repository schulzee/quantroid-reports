Gross margin has inflected from roughly -93% in 2024 to +14.3% TTM, and cash burn has narrowed to ~$41mm, but the company still loses money, carries ~$295mm of convertible notes against ~$131mm of liquidity, and prints at an outlier ~11.5x EV/revenue. The outcome is binary: if the software pivot scales at high margin before the 2028 convert maturity, the equity re-rates; if it stalls, dilution or restructuring follows.   • US battery-storage deployments + solar additions (the direct end-market; storage is co-bid with solar): The sector's own end-demand is utility-scale BESS GW/GWh installed, and a large share is procured alongside solar (hybrid PV+storage), so solar additions are an independent pull on storage attach. This is the most direct checkable demand metric (published by EIA/WoodMac/SEIA), separate from any single company's commentary.
  • Federal storage-ITC durability + tariff/FEOC regime (post-2025 OBBBA) — sets project IRRs and supply-chain winners: Standalone-storage project economics hinge on the federal ITC; the IRA created a tech-neutral 48E credit for storage. The 2025 reconciliation law (OBBBA) reshaped clean-energy credits but treated storage far more favorably than wind/solar, while new foreign-entity-of-concern (FEOC) and content rules plus Section 301 tariffs on Chinese cells advantage US-made (EOSE zinc, FLNC US modules) over import-dependent project economics. This is a checkable statutory/tariff driver, not management spin.
  • Interconnection-queue backlog & storage's share of the queue: Storage is the single largest category in US interconnection queues and deploys faster than gas/transmission; queue volume and storage share are an independent, published proxy for forward developer intent that leads deployments ~4 years. The bottleneck is increasingly interconnection/transmission throughput, not demand.

Structural shifts in play:

  • Structural inflection in US electricity demand after ~15 flat years (AI/data-center load + electrification + onshoring) — the secular force re-rating power infrastructure broadly (direction: tailwind; horizon: 3-10y; impact: high) — EPRI 'Powering Intelligence 2026' projects data centers reach 9-17% of US electricity by 2030 (from 4-5%), a ~60% upward revision vs its 2024 estimate; S&P Global sees data-center grid demand +22% in 2025 and near-tripling by 2030; EIA now plans a record ~86 GW of 2026 capacity additions. New load creates resource-adequacy/firming demand that fast-to-deploy BESS fills ahead of gas (multi-year turbine backlogs) and transmission, and data centers increasingly contract storage directly — permanently widening the storage TAM beyond renewable-firming.
  • Post-OBBBA policy bifurcation: storage ITC retained while wind/solar accelerated, plus FEOC/Section 301 tariffs reshaping supply toward US-made cells — with a near-term 2026 air-pocket risk (direction: tailwind; horizon: 1-3y; impact: high) — OBBBA kept the tech-neutral 48E storage ITC on the IRA's long runway (full credit if construction begins by Dec 31, 2033) while accelerating wind/solar sunset (placed-in-service by 2027), and added FEOC/material-assistance limits; the Section 301 tariff on Chinese lithium-ion grid batteries rose to 25% on Jan 1, 2026 (from 7.5%). This structurally favors US-made/non-Chinese supply (EOSE, FLNC) but is a near-term headwind to import-dependent IRRs — Wood Mackenzie warns the US grid-scale BESS segment could shrink ~by a third in 2026 on tariffs/FEOC even as 2026-2031 cumulative storage rises ~250%, after a 2025 pull-forward to lock prior ITC rules.
  • Global LFP/Li-ion cell-cost deflation (Chinese overcapacity) lowering system $/kWh and expanding the viable TAM — blunted domestically by US tariffs (direction: tailwind; horizon: 1-3y; impact: med) — Per BloombergNEF's annual survey, lithium-ion pack prices fell sharply (to roughly the low-$100s/kWh range, the largest drop since 2017) on Chinese cell/metal overcapacity, cutting global BESS system costs and enlarging the economically viable TAM — a demand tailwind. But US Section 301 tariffs (grid-battery line to 25% Jan 2026), AD/CVD actions and FEOC rules blunt that deflation domestically, creating a two-tier market that favors US manufacturing yet pressures integrator margins (the thin ~10-13% gross margins FLNC reports) and near-term US project IRRs.
  • Emergence of long-duration energy storage (LDES) and non-lithium chemistries (iron-flow GWH, zinc EOSE) as grids exceed lithium's ~4-hour economic sweet spot (direction: tailwind; horizon: 3-10y; impact: med) — As renewable penetration and multi-hour data-center firming needs rise, grids increasingly need 8-12h+ duration that lithium serves poorly on cost, opening a distinct LDES TAM supported by DOE's Long Duration Storage Shot (target ~90% cost reduction for 10+ hour systems) and OCED LDES demonstration funding, plus state LDES procurement targets (CA, NY). This is the key differentiator for GWH (iron-flow) and partly EOSE (zinc, 3-12h). Risk: LDES cost-down/bankability is unproven at scale (GWH carries going-concern doubt), and cheap lithium may stretch into longer durations and cap the niche.

The question that decides the thesis: Does the post-2020 US power-demand inflection (data-center/AI load growth) plus the retained tech-neutral storage ITC convert the record interconnection-queue backlog into actual contracted BESS deployments fast enough — and at high enough margins under the new 25% Section 301 tariff + FEOC regime — to carry these pre-profit names to self-funding profitability before the 2026 tariff/FEOC air-pocket (WoodMac's ~one-third grid-scale contraction) and their cash runways force dilutive financing?

Synthesis & verdict

Stem entered 2026 as a deliberately shrunken business. Through 2022-2023 it booked large hardware-resale revenue (peaking at $461.6mm in 2023) at near-zero or negative gross margin, then exited that activity, collapsing revenue to $144.3mm in 2024 and ~$132.5mm TTM while gross margin swung from roughly -93% in 2024 (driven by inventory and asset writedowns) to +14.3% TTM. The strategic logic is to become an asset-light, high-margin energy-software company built on Athena (storage optimization across a large installed GWh base) and AlsoEnergy's PowerTrack (solar asset management). The repositioning is coherent, but the high-margin software business is still small, and the market is being asked to pay an outlier ~11.5x EV/revenue for the option that it scales.

The financial reality is that this remains a cash-consuming venture with no positive earnings stream. Operating income is -$71.2mm and net income -$94.8mm on a TTM basis; book equity turned negative (-$98.6mm) in 2024 after an ~$851.8mm goodwill/intangible impairment that effectively wrote down much of the AlsoEnergy acquisition. Because there is no positive normalized earnings power and no tangible asset floor, intrinsic-value methods (DCF, EPV, Graham net-worth) cannot be meaningfully applied. The investable question is purely venture: does revenue re-accelerate on software, does gross margin keep climbing, and does cash burn cross into positive territory before financing pressure forces dilution.

The balance sheet defines the clock. Cash plus short-term investments of ~$131mm sit against ~$295mm of convertible notes due 2028 and 2030, with net debt of ~$164mm. Free cash flow is -$41.1mm TTM, an improvement from -$82.9mm of operating cash burn in 2023, implying roughly three years of runway at the current pace. Management has bought back and exchanged converts below face to cut leverage, which is constructive, but a 1-for-20 reverse split in late 2024 to hold the NYSE listing and steady share-count growth (137.5mm in 2021 to ~167.5mm TTM) underscore the dilution history. The central tension is that the ~3-year runway roughly coincides with the 2028 convert maturity, so the pivot must produce positive EBITDA on a fairly tight timeline or a refinancing/raise becomes likely.

Within the grid-scale storage cohort, Stem occupies an awkward middle. Fluence offers scale (~$2.7B revenue), a net-cash balance sheet, and thin-but-positive-trending margins at under 1x EV/revenue; Eos is a pre-scale, high-torque manufacturing ramp. Stem is neither cheap-and-safe nor a clean early-stage manufacturing bet: it is a software-pivot call wrapped in a fragile balance sheet at a rich multiple. Sector tailwinds (IRA standalone-storage ITC, AI/data-center grid load, double-digit storage deployment growth) are real, but they do not guarantee firm-level survival, and software differentiation against Fluence Mosaic and Tesla Autobidder is unproven. The risk/reward is binary and better suited to small, loss-tolerant speculation than to value or quality capital.
